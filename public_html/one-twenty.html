When I Was One-and-Twenty
The poem "When I Was One-and-Twenty" talks about the naivety of youth and how young people frequently ignore the advise of those who are older and probably wiser.
When I was one-and-twenty
I heard a wise man say,

“Give crowns and pounds and guineas
But not your heart away;
Give pearls away and rubies
But keep your fancy free.”
But I was one-and-twenty,
No use to talk to me.

When I was one-and-twenty
I heard him say again,
“The heart out of the bosom
Was never given in vain;
’Tis paid with sighs a plenty
And sold for endless rue.”
And I am two-and-twenty,
And oh, ’tis true, ’tis true.

A.E. Housman, full name Alfred Edward Housman, was an English scholar and poet whose poems expressed Romantic pessimism in a plain, straightforward style.

The ‘Loveliest of trees, the cherry now’, this is one of E. Housman's most extensively anthologized poems, with the speaker ruminating on the reality that, at the age of 20, he only has 50 of his threescore and ten years left.
